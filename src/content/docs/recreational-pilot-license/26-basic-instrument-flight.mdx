CASA Recreational Pilot License (Aeroplane) — CASA Sample Syllabus Lesson 26.

This lesson introduces flying the aeroplane accurately by sole reference to the instruments — straight and level, climbing, descending and turning — and teaches how to recognise and recover from an unusual attitude. It also covers the sensory illusions that can mislead a pilot without outside visual reference, and the actions to take if you ever find yourself in cloud unexpectedly. Alongside the new instrument work, this flight revises and assesses a long list of foundational skills: communication, pre-flight actions and inspection, start/stop, taxiing, pre-take-off procedures, and radio/transponder operation.

This lesson pairs its theory brief with a self-paced **pre-lesson workbook** — complete
it in your own time before the lesson. For each of four topics (instrument power
sources, instrument checks and serviceability, instrument lag, and compass turning and
acceleration errors) it curates a short video and article, then asks a few questions for
you to answer in the space provided. It takes about 30–45 minutes, and we'll check
through your answers together at the start of the pre-flight brief.

to print or fill in on your device.

## Theory Brief

The live session builds on the workbook: how to read the panel as a system (the
control-and-performance method, and a continuous scan), the sensory illusions that can
mislead a pilot in cloud, avoiding and escaping inadvertent IMC, and recognising and
recovering from a nose-high or nose-low unusual attitude. View the slides on this page
below or [open the slides](/open-aviation-briefings/brief-slides/recreational-pilot-license/26-basic-instrument-flight/01-theory.html)

The pre-flight brief (~15 min, immediately before the flight) works best as an
interactive **whiteboard session**, not slides — stepping through the flight plan, the
panel grouping, the recovery drills and the threats together with the student. These
printable notes give you the briefing components (aim, today's flight walk-through,
threats, and a trust-the-instruments airmanship emphasis with a recovery rehearsal) with
prompts and fill-in blanks, plus a front page to sketch your own whiteboard plan and
running order. Although it will be helpful to plan and step through it yourself before
the lesson, remember: **modelling the process** of the pre-flight brief — especially the
recovery rehearsal — matters more than presenting a polished brief. This is why we do it
interactively rather than as a presentation.

## Useful resources for students

- The NZ Civil Aviation Authority has whiteboards on [Instrument flying introduction](https://www.aviation.govt.nz/assets/licensing-and-certification/flight-instructor-guide/whiteboards/fig-whiteboard-if-ifi.pdf),
  [Unusual attitudes](https://www.aviation.govt.nz/assets/licensing-and-certification/flight-instructor-guide/whiteboards/fig-whiteboard-if-ua.pdf)
  and [Compass use](https://www.aviation.govt.nz/assets/licensing-and-certification/flight-instructor-guide/whiteboards/fig-whiteboard-if-cu.pdf)
  that summarise the technique in the NZ CAA Flight Instructor Guide style — suitable for the student to download and study before the flight.
- The FAA [Pilot's Handbook of Aeronautical Knowledge](https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/handbooks_manuals/aviation/phak)
  Chapter 8 (Flight Instruments) covers the pitot-static and gyroscopic instruments and the magnetic compass in detail, and Chapter 17
  (Aeromedical Factors) covers spatial disorientation and sensory illusions.
- The ATSB's [VFR into IMC](https://www.atsb.gov.au/taxonomy/term/385) topic page collects Australian investigations and occurrence
  briefs on this exact hazard, including a January 2025 non-fatal case ([AO-2025-003](https://www.atsb.gov.au/investigations/ao-2025-003))
  where insufficient weather planning and self-imposed pressure led a VFR pilot into cloud shortly after take-off.

## Example email to students before the lesson

If you are an instructor, feel free to modify this for your own use. Sending an email a few
days before a lesson is a great way to engage people in the learning before they even arrive
(if they have time and capacity).

> **Subject:** Lesson 26 — Basic instrument flight
>
> Hi [name],
>
> This lesson is about flying the aeroplane using only the instruments — no outside
> visual reference at all. It might sound daunting, but it's genuinely one of the more
> fascinating lessons: you'll be surprised how convincingly your senses can mislead you
> once the horizon disappears, and how much you can trust the panel instead. We'll also
> practise recognising and recovering from an unusual attitude, and talk through what to
> do if you ever find yourself in cloud by mistake.
>
> Before the flight, there's a short **pre-lesson workbook** [1] to work through in your
> own time (about 30–45 minutes) — a few short videos and articles with some questions
> to answer, covering the instrument panel itself. Bring your answers to the lesson. The
> live theory brief then builds on that (about 35 minutes), and we'll fly for about an
> hour, with roughly 35 minutes of that under the hood or foggles.
>
> **When:** [day]
>
> See you then!
> [1] https://open-aviation-solutions.github.io/open-aviation-briefings/recreational-pilot-license/26-basic-instrument-flight/
