# What if you couldn't see the horizon?

One of the more dangerous possibilities in a visual flight is that you fly into clouds and, as a result, **lose your horizontal reference - the horizon**.

Our inner ear's balance sense can be tricked very convincingly once our eyes lose a fixed reference point:
 - We can feel that we're going up when really we're accelerating (dangerous if you "correct" and are low to the ground),
 - We can feel that we're flying wings-level when we're actually banking.

How we avoid being tricked is what this lesson is about.

# So how do we fly without it?

We already have instruments that show an artificial horizon, airspeed, height and heading. Today we'll be learning:

- How to read them **as a system**, not one at a time
- Why your **senses can lie to you** once the horizon is gone
- What to do if you ever find yourself **in cloud unexpectedly**
- How to recognise and recover from an **unusual attitude**

> Trust the instruments over your senses.

# Two ways to read the panel: control and performance

- **Control instruments** — tell you what you've *set*: the **Attitude Indicator** (pitch and bank) and the **power indicators** (tachometer / manifold pressure)
- **Performance instruments** — tell you the *result*: airspeed, altitude, rate of climb or descent, heading, balance

> Set the control instruments to a known attitude and power combination, then confirm the result on the performance instruments.

# Scan technique — don't stare, don't chase

We'll use a simple hub-and-spoke scan technique:

- Keep your eyes **moving** — fixating on any one instrument is a fixation *away* from all the others
- A simple scan: **Attitude Indicator in the centre** (the hub), glance out to each performance instrument (the spokes) and back
- Make a **small** correction, then **wait** for the aircraft to settle before judging the result — chasing an instrument that's still lagging (remember your workbook) only makes the oscillation worse
- CASA calls this **scan technique appropriate to the fitted instruments and phase of flight** [IFF 4(a)]

# Why your senses can lie to you

Hopefully you've already watched the excellent [Spatial Disorientation video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XrE07yekPn4) from Gold Seal Flight Training as part of your workbook exercise. To summarise:

- Your inner ear (the vestibular system) senses **rotation and acceleration** — not attitude — and it's easily fooled once you can't see the horizon
- A slow, sustained turn can stop feeling like a turn at all
- Rapid acceleration can feel like a climb; deceleration can feel like a descent

> If your senses and your instruments disagree, **believe the instruments**.

---

# Three illusions worth knowing

| Illusion | Feels like | What's actually happening |
|---|---|---|
| **The leans** | Banked when you're level (or level when you're banked) | A slow or prolonged turn stops registering in the inner ear; levelling out feels like banking the other way |
| **Graveyard spiral** | A gentle, level turn — no sensation of banking | A prolonged turn desensitises the inner ear while the aircraft is actually descending steeply and tightening |
| **Somatogravic illusion** | A strong pitch-up (climbing) sensation | Rapid acceleration (full power, or a go-around) is felt as a climb, tempting a dangerous nose-down correction |

For another deeper dive on the Leans illusion, see the Flight Club video [The LEANS ILLUSION. How pilots get disoriented.](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5ZG17H8yizI).

# The only reliable fix

- **Trust the instruments over your senses**, every time you're without outside visual reference
- Illusions are strongest when your **head moves** while turning (looking down at a chart, reaching for something) — keep head movements smooth and minimal under the hood
- If a sensation and an instrument disagree, believe the instrument — and **say it out loud**; naming it helps break the illusion's grip

> Believing your senses over the instruments is how a perfectly serviceable aeroplane flies into the ground. It has happened, repeatedly, to experienced pilots.

# The danger of VFR flight into cloud

- As a VFR pilot, you are not trained or endorsed to fly solely on instruments in real cloud
- Without outside reference, the illusions we just covered can develop within **seconds**
- VFR-into-IMC is one of the most consistently fatal categories of general aviation accident — about **1 in every 9** reported occurrences in Australia (2015–2025) resulted in a fatality

> The safest response to deteriorating weather is the one made **before** you're in it: turn back, or land, while you still have the choice.

# Avoiding it in the first place

- Check the **actual and forecast weather** thoroughly before you fly — not just at departure, but along the route and at any alternate
- Set **personal minimums** (cloud base, visibility) in advance, and stick to them under pressure
- Identify **escape routes and alternates** during planning, not in the moment
- Recognise **external pressure** to press on ("get-there-itis") as a threat in itself

> A non-fatal but telling case: a pilot flying Bankstown to Orange in January 2025 entered cloud shortly after take-off — ATSB found insufficient pre-flight weather consideration and self-imposed pressure to proceed both contributed. [ATSB AO-2025-003](https://www.atsb.gov.au/investigations/ao-2025-003)

# If you find yourself in cloud anyway

The standard actions, **in order**:

1. **Maintain control** — wings level, on the instruments, immediately
2. **Turn back** — a gentle rate-one turn onto a reciprocal heading, back the way you came, toward known VMC
3. **Tell someone** — a radio call, and the transponder code briefed for the situation
4. **Descend only if safe** — only if you know the terrain and it will genuinely help; otherwise hold height and turn first

> The turn back is the single most important action. Most inadvertent-IMC accidents happen because the pilot pressed on instead of turning around immediately.

# Recognising an unusual attitude

Two broad types, told apart mainly by the **airspeed**:

| | **Nose-high** | **Nose-low** |
|---|---|---|
| **Airspeed** | Low and **decreasing** | High and **increasing** |
| **Altimeter** | Increasing | Decreasing |
| **VSI** | Climbing | Descending |
| **Danger** | Approaching the stall | Overspeed / spiral dive |

> Airspeed is your fastest clue: decreasing means nose-high, increasing means nose-low.

<!--

This connects to Lesson 18's spiral dive comparison table — same discriminator (the
ASI), same underlying idea. Repeat that link out loud if the student flew Lesson 18.

-->

---

# Recovery, in order

**Nose-high (low or decreasing airspeed):**
1. **Power** — apply full power
2. **Roll** the wings level
3. **Ease forward** to lower the nose to the level-flight attitude
4. Once settled, adjust power and re-trim

**Nose-low (high or increasing airspeed):**
1. **Power** — close the throttle
2. **Roll** the wings level
3. **Ease back** smoothly to raise the nose to the horizon — no snatch
4. Once settled, resume cruise power and re-trim

> **Power, then wings level, then pitch** — in that order, for either case. Rolling level before you pitch is what stops a nose-low recovery turning into a spiral dive (Lesson 18).

<!--

FIM Ch 17 background: nose-high recovery is simultaneous full power + roll wings level +
ease forward; nose-low recovery is close throttle (if fast) + roll wings level, then ease
out of the dive. This is the same "wings level before you pitch" principle taught for the
spiral dive in Lesson 18 — deliberately repeat it here.

-->

# Flying it on the full panel

- Today we fly with the **full instrument panel** available — every instrument working, cross-referenced continuously
- The Attitude Indicator gives the fastest, most direct picture of the unusual attitude — but always **confirm** with the ASI, altimeter and VSI, since the AI can topple past its limits (typically 60° pitch / 90° roll on this class of aircraft)
- Ignore the sensations — they will likely disagree with what the instruments show. **Believe the instruments.**
